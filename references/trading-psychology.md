# Trading Psychology Reference
last_verified: 2026-03-22

## Overview

Trading psychology is the invisible edge. Two traders with the same strategy will have vastly different results based on their mental discipline. This reference provides frameworks for identifying psychological pitfalls and protocols for maintaining peak performance.

---

## Cognitive Biases in Trading

### Loss Aversion
- **What it is**: Losses feel 2-2.5x more painful than equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman & Tversky)
- **How it manifests**: Holding losing trades too long ("it'll come back"), cutting winners too early ("lock in the profit before it disappears"), skipping valid setups after a loss
- **Antidote**: Pre-define stop and target BEFORE entering. Use the partial profit system (50% at 1R, etc.) to mechanically manage exits. Accept that losses are a business expense.

### Confirmation Bias
- **What it is**: Seeking information that confirms your existing belief, ignoring contradicting evidence
- **How it manifests**: You are long ES and only notice bullish signals, ignoring bearish divergences and resistance levels
- **Antidote**: Before every trade, explicitly write down what would INVALIDATE your thesis. Actively look for reasons you are wrong. If you cannot find any, you are not looking hard enough.

### Recency Bias
- **What it is**: Overweighting recent events relative to the broader dataset
- **How it manifests**: After 3 winning trades, feeling invincible (increasing size). After 3 losers, feeling the strategy is broken (abandoning it).
- **Antidote**: Judge your strategy over 50-100+ trades, not the last 3-5. Keep a running stats tracker. Short-term results are noise.

### Anchoring
- **What it is**: Fixating on a specific price or number as a reference point
- **How it manifests**: "ES was at 5200 last week, so 5150 is cheap" (ignoring that the structure is bearish). Or anchoring to your entry price and refusing to exit until price returns to it.
- **Antidote**: Trade what the chart shows NOW, not what it showed before. Your entry price is irrelevant to where the market is going.

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
- **What it is**: Continuing a course of action because of past investment (time, money, ego) rather than future expected value
- **How it manifests**: "I've already lost $500 on this trade, I can't close it now." Or staying in a failed strategy because you spent months developing it.
- **Antidote**: Every decision should be forward-looking. Ask: "If I had no position right now, would I enter this trade at this price in this direction?" If no, close it.

### Gambler's Fallacy
- **What it is**: Believing past random events influence future random events ("I'm due for a winner")
- **How it manifests**: After 5 losses, increasing size because "the next one HAS to win." Each trade is independent.
- **Antidote**: Each trade is statistically independent. Your win rate plays out over hundreds of trades, not the next one. After losses, REDUCE size, do not increase it.

### Overconfidence
- **What it is**: Overestimating your knowledge, skill, or edge
- **How it manifests**: After a winning streak, increasing position size beyond the plan. Skipping analysis because "I can feel the market." Trading without a stop because "I know where it's going."
- **Antidote**: The market humbles everyone. Stick to your position sizing formula regardless of recent results. Winning streaks end. Always use a stop.

## Revenge Trading

### What It Is
Taking a trade to "get back" losses rather than because the setup warrants it. It is the single most destructive behavior in trading.

### Triggers
- Anger or frustration after a losing trade
- The thought "I need to make it back"
- Increasing position size to recover faster
- Entering without a proper setup just to "be in the market"
- Switching from your primary timeframe to a lower one to find more "opportunities"

### Prevention Protocol
1. **Recognize the trigger**: The moment you think "I need to get it back" -- STOP. That is the revenge signal.
2. **Close the platform**: Not minimize. Close entirely. Remove the temptation.
3. **15-minute mandatory break**: Walk away from the desk. Physically leave the room if needed.
4. **Review the loss**: Open your diary. Write down what happened. Was the loss a valid stop-out on a good setup (acceptable) or a mistake (something to fix)?
5. **Re-entry criteria**: Return ONLY if: (a) you feel calm, (b) there is a valid A+ setup, (c) you have reduced your position size by 50%.
6. **If still emotional**: The trading day is OVER. No exceptions.

## FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)

### The Truth About FOMO
- **"There is always another setup."** The market will be here tomorrow.
- Missing a trade costs $0. A bad trade costs money AND discipline.
- The trade you missed always looks better than it was. You are seeing the result, not the uncertain decision point.

### FOMO Triggers
- Seeing price run without you after you hesitated
- Social media showing other traders' winners
- A long period without a trade (feeling like you "should" be trading)

### FOMO Antidote
1. You do NOT need to trade every day. Some of the best days are no-trade days.
2. If you missed a move, do NOT chase it. Wait for the next pullback or the next setup.
3. Remove real-time P&L display if it triggers emotional responses
4. Unfollow or mute traders on social media who post unrealistic P&L screenshots
5. Your job is to execute YOUR plan, not to catch every move in the market

## Discipline Framework

### Core Principle: Process Over Outcome

A disciplined trade that loses money is a SUCCESS (you followed your rules).
An undisciplined trade that makes money is a FAILURE (you got lucky and reinforced bad habits).

### The Only Question That Matters
After every trade, ask: **"Did I follow my rules?"**
- Did I wait for a valid setup? (Yes/No)
- Did I enter at my planned price? (Yes/No)
- Was my stop placed correctly? (Yes/No)
- Did I manage partials according to plan? (Yes/No)
- Did I let the trade play out? (Yes/No)

If all answers are "Yes" -- the trade was a success, regardless of P&L.
If any answer is "No" -- the trade was a failure, regardless of P&L.

### Grading System
- **A**: Followed all rules. Entered on a valid setup with proper risk.
- **B**: Followed most rules. Minor deviation (e.g., slightly early entry).
- **C**: Deviated from plan. Wrong setup, wrong size, or emotional entry.
- **D**: Full rule violation. Revenge trade, no stop, excessive size.

Track your grade distribution. Target: 80%+ A/B trades. If C/D trades exceed 20%, stop live trading and address the issue.

## Tilt Detection Checklist

Tilt is the trading equivalent of poker tilt -- emotional compromise that degrades decision-making. Run this checklist after any loss.

### Physical Signs
- [ ] Elevated heart rate or shallow breathing
- [ ] Clenching jaw, fists, or tensing shoulders
- [ ] Feeling hot or flushed
- [ ] Restless, unable to sit still

### Mental Signs
- [ ] Wanting to "prove something" to the market
- [ ] Thinking "I need to make it back"
- [ ] Wanting to increase position size
- [ ] Feeling certain about the next trade (overconfidence after loss)
- [ ] Rationalizing a setup that you would normally skip

### Behavioral Signs
- [ ] Deviating from the trading plan
- [ ] Taking B/C setups instead of waiting for A+
- [ ] Checking P&L repeatedly
- [ ] Switching to lower timeframes to find more entries
- [ ] Talking to yourself in angry or frustrated tones

### If 3+ items are checked: You are on tilt. Activate the Kill Switch Protocol immediately.

## Pre-Trade Ritual

Execute this ritual EVERY TIME before placing a trade. It takes 15 seconds and prevents impulsive entries.

### The 5-Step Ritual
1. **PAUSE**: 5-second pause. Hands off the keyboard. Breathe.
2. **NAME IT**: Say the setup out loud: "This is a bullish order block entry at the 62% fib in the NY Kill Zone, aligned with the 4H uptrend."
3. **CONFIRM R:R**: "My stop is X ticks, my target is Y ticks. That is Z:1 R:R." (Must be >= 2:1)
4. **CONFIRM RISK**: "I am risking N contracts, which is $X, which is Y% of my account." (Must be <= 1%)
5. **EXECUTE**: Place the order. Set the stop. Set the target. Walk away from the screen.

If you cannot complete steps 2-4 clearly and calmly, you do not have a valid trade. Walk away.

## Drawdown Recovery Protocol

### When to Activate
- Account is down 5%+ from peak
- 3+ consecutive losing days
- Feeling of desperation or hopelessness about the account

### Recovery Steps

#### Phase 1: Stabilize (Days 1-3)
- Reduce position size to **25% of normal** (e.g., if you trade 4 ES, trade 1 MES)
- Only take A+ setups with minimum 3:1 R:R
- Maximum 2 trades per day
- Goal: Stop the bleeding. Build a small green day.

#### Phase 2: Rebuild (Days 4-10)
- Increase to 50% of normal size after 2 consecutive green days
- Still only A+ setups
- Maximum 3 trades per day
- Goal: String together consistent small wins

#### Phase 3: Restore (Days 11+)
- Return to 75% size after reaching 50% recovery of the drawdown
- Expand to A and B setups
- Normal trade frequency
- Return to 100% size after full recovery or after 3 consecutive green weeks

### Key Rules During Recovery
- Do NOT try to recover all at once. That is the revenge trading trap.
- Small wins rebuild confidence and skill. Large bets rebuild nothing.
- If at any point during recovery you have another -2% day, restart at Phase 1.
- Journaling is mandatory during recovery. Every trade must be documented.

## Daily Mental Preparation

### Morning Routine (Before Market Open)
1. Check your emotional state: Am I rested? Am I stressed? Am I calm?
2. Review the daily economic calendar
3. Review yesterday's diary entry (what went well, what to improve)
4. Mark key levels on your charts (no trading yet)
5. Set your daily risk limit and write it down
6. State your intention: "Today I will follow my rules and take only A+ setups."

### End-of-Day Routine (After Market Close)
1. Complete the diary entry for the day
2. Grade each trade (A/B/C/D)
3. Calculate daily P&L (including commissions)
4. Identify one thing done well and one thing to improve
5. Close all charts and trading platforms
6. Disconnect from markets until next morning
